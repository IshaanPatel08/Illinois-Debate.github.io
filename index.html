ILLINOIS DEBATE COMMUNITY

Start here to learn about high school debate in Illinois

What is competitive high school debate?

Debate is an academic sport that uses arguments to earn points and win rounds. All competitors receive some topics for debate about one month before a tournament. In the weeks leading up to a tournament, each school’s debate team prepares by learning everything they can about a topic, brainstorming arguments that could be made on each side, finding expert sources for support, writing speeches, and practicing by debating each other. Tournaments are held on Saturdays at different high schools. All the schools who have been preparing to debate the topic(s) will compete against each other at the host high school. The debaters who do the best job persuading their judges win awards, and schools with many strong debaters compete for team sweepstakes awards.

What types of debate can I do in Illinois?

  • One-versus-one (Lincoln-Douglas Debate or LD): Two students from different schools debate against each other, and an adult judge decides the winner. Topics for LD Debate pose ethical questions, so debaters read traditional and contemporary political philosophy in addition to topic-specific literature. LD Debate topics last for two months during the school year (September/October, November/December, January/February, March/April). Debaters with the most wins earn trophies.
  • Two-versus-two (Public Forum Debate or PF): Two students from the same school debate against two students from a different school, and an adult judge decides the winner. Topics for PF Debate pose current events questions, so debaters read recent texts from newspapers, magazines, and academic journals. PF topics last for two months at the beginning of the season and then move to a one month rotation (September/October, November/December, January, February, March, April). Debaters with the most wins earn trophies.
  • Two-versus-two (Policy Debate): Two students from the same school debate against two students from a different school, and an adult judge decides the winner. Policy Debate uses the same topic for the entire school year, so students must become experts on it. Debaters with the most wins earn trophies.
  • Congressional Debate: 16-20 students form a mock House of Representatives in a classroom. Those debaters take turns speaking for and against legislation, which is proposed a month prior to the tournament. A student presiding officer uses Robert’s Rules of Order to make sure that everyone gets an equal opportunity to speak. Adult judges score speeches, write constructive feedback, and rank the debaters. The highest ranked debaters win medals and trophies. Teams compete for sweepstakes awards.
  • Pro/Con Debate Challenge: Students select a topic and create pro and con speeches for it. They record themselves delivering their speeches and then upload them to a tournament website. Adult judges evaluate the recorded speeches on their own time and rank them. Pro/Con Debate Challenge is the only form of debate that does not require any travel to other schools. The highest ranked debaters win medals and trophies.
